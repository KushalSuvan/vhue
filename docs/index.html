Devolving Microscopic Priority to Mesoscopic Cells

Notes on Traffic Flow Theory — Mathematical Foundations

Abstract: This text establishes the rigorous proofs required to bridge microscopic priority vehicle dynamics to a mesoscopic priority cell structure. By enforcing a strict "no overtake" assumption, we align individual vehicle trajectories with the macroscopic flow conserved by the Lighthill-Whitham-Richards (LWR) model and discretized via Daganzo's Cell Transmission Model.

1. The Conservation Law

Before introducing priority parameters, we must establish the foundational continuous model. Let $\rho(x,t)$ denote the traffic density and $q(x,t)$ the flow at space $x$ and time $t$. The standard LWR model is dictated by the scalar conservation law:

$$ \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial q}{\partial x} = 0 $$

Assuming a fundamental diagram $q = Q(\rho)$, where $Q$ is a concave function representing the equilibrium flow-density relationship, we can define the wave speed as the derivative of the fundamental diagram, $c(\rho) = Q'(\rho)$.

2. Daganzo's Discretization

To transition this PDE into a computationally viable mesoscopic state, we utilize the Cell Transmission Model (CTM). The highway is partitioned into cells $i$ of length $\Delta x$, and time into intervals $k$ of duration $\Delta t$. The update equation for cell occupancy $n_i^k$ is:

$$ n_i^{k+1} = n_i^k + y_{i-1}^k - y_i^k $$

Where $y_i^k$ is the flow across the boundary between cell $i$ and $i+1$. By enforcing a strict First-In-First-Out (FIFO) constraint—the mathematical equivalent of a "no overtake" rule—we prevent pathological vehicle jumping and maintain the conservation bounded by maximum flow and jam density limits.

3. Global Route Probability Distributions

To operate effectively, the environment must account for vehicles traversing predefined networks. It is a well-established property of Dynamic Origin-Destination (OD) estimation that, on a stochastic scale, network routing probabilities shift structurally over the hours of the day based on demand elasticity.

At the intersection level, this macroscopic phenomenon translates directly to shifting probabilities over turn directions. We can conceptualize this as temporal phases of routing: at hour one, we observe a higher probability of left-turn routes; at hour two, the distribution shifts heavily toward right-turn routes.

Crucially, temporal correlation analyses of traffic links demonstrate that there is little to no statistical correlation between these distinct phases. A practical mechanism driving this decoupling is the independence of trip purposes. For instance, school transit heavily populates the 07:00–08:00 window, whereas commercial office transit dominates the 08:00–09:00 window. Because the spatial loci of these two destinations are fundamentally separate, the transition between them lacks strong temporal correlation and can be modeled as a multivariate white noise process.

Thus, to accurately reflect this temporal decoupling in the environment, the model randomly assigns updated global route probability distributions at discrete simulation step intervals.
